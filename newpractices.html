New SQA Practice

I recommend that we, at TravelWare, introduce the idea of metrics to the company. The SQA department will be making sure that this new practice is implemented within the whole company.

Metrics
A metric can be defined as a standard of measuring or evaluating something. A measure is a quantity, a proportion or a qualitative comparison of some kind. There are 3 different types of metrics:

  1. Informational - Will tell us what is going on
  2. Diagnostic - Will identify areas for improvement
  3. Motivational - Will influence behaviour

Metrics have always been used to help guide managers with decisions about the organisation. Introducing metrics to TravelWare will provide the CIO, the senior management and the project managers how to effectively and efficiently manage the software development department of the company. Although firstly, we need to ask each other some questions. Is defect tracking necessary? Who should decide which metrics are the most useful to the company? How can a team get the most value out of its quality metrics program?

Why Metrics?
Metrics or measures allow for the interpretation and analysis of data. Metrics can be used to predict or estimate how long a project is going to take from start to finish. It can provide a status on a project at any time. It will also be able to tell if there are any implications in the project or process. It can assess the software development process itself and metrics will also be able to tell TravelWare if it has the correct staffing needs within the company.

Data are the actual, factual units of information that are measured directly at some point in the process. Example:

  • Units produced
  • Lines of code in a module
  • User Stories/Use cases
  • Defects Found/Reported/Corrected
  • Tests Completed/Passed

The key issue is to make sure that TravelWare collect accurate data. This data must be validated and verified at the point of collection. The data definitions must be precise so that we know exactly what to collect and report. Imprecise data definitions can lead to useless data and it could mean wasting time that company doesn't have. The company will be able to use, display and communicate these metrics so that the employees and clients will not distrust how they are being used.

Primitive Metrics
Primitive metrics or raw data are the best things to maintain in a historical database. They are often less biased. They can have many different uses and are typically used in many calculations for different measures. On the downside, primitive metrics take up a lot of storage although data reduction techniques can help.

Calculated Metrics
Calculated metrics are computed or calculated values that depend on data or other measures. Example:

  • Defects per line of code
  • Defects per hour of Testing
  • Correction Time per defect
  • Mean Time between failure

What will TravelWare be measuring?
We at TravelWare will be measuring a number of different things through the software development process. We will be introducing 3 metrics to begin with.

  • Size - LOC
  • Complexity - McCabe's Cyclomatic Complexity Metric
  • Structure - LCOM

What will TravelWare not be measuring?
The SQA department at TravelWare will be not measuring a few different areas of data. We will not be measuring the productivity or performance of each individual. If we were to do this, it would cause people to generate invalid numbers and it can easily penalise that most valuable contributors to each project. Instead, we will be putting the focus on the process. This will allow us to measure the product and the information to help improve the process and it will help us measure people by their mature and effective use of a process to help improve overall results.

Lines Of Code

Source lines of code (SLOC or LOC) is a software metric used to measure the size of a software program by counting the number of lines in the text of the program's source code. SLOC is typically used to predict the amount of effort that will be required to develop a program, as well as to estimate programming productivity or effort once the software is produced.

There are two major types of SLOC measures: physical SLOC (LOC) and logical SLOC (LLOC). Specific definitions of these two measures vary, but the most common definition of physical SLOC is a count of lines in the text of the program's source code including comment lines. Blank lines are also included unless the lines of code in a section consists of more than 25% blank lines. In this case blank lines in excess of 25% are not counted toward lines of code.

Logical LOC attempts to measure the number of "statements", but their specific definitions are tied to specific computer languages (one simple logical LOC measure for C-like programming languages is the number of statement-terminating semicolons). It is much easier to create tools that measure physical SLOC, and physical SLOC definitions are easier to explain. However, physical SLOC measures are sensitive to logically irrelevant formatting and style conventions, while logical LOC is less sensitive to formatting and style conventions. Unfortunately, SLOC measures are often stated without giving their definition, and logical LOC can often be significantly different from physical SLOC.

McCabe's Cyclomatic Complexity Metric

A measure of the complexity of a program was developed by [McCabe 1976]. He developed a system which he called the cyclomatic complexity of a program. This system measures the number of independent paths in a program, thereby placing a numerical value on the complexity. In practice it is a count of the number of test conditions in a program.

The cyclomatic complexity (CC) of a graph (G) may be computed according to the following formula:
CC(G) = Number (edges) - Number (nodes) + 1

The results of multiple experiments (G.A. Miller) suggest that modules approach zero defects when McCabe's Cyclomatic Complexity is within 7 Â± 2.

A study of PASCAL and FORTRAN programs (Lind and Vairavan 1989) found that a Cyclomatic Complexity between 10 and 15 minimized the number of module changes.

Lack of Cohesion of Methods

LCOM measures the degree of similarity of methods by instance variables. Any measure of separateness of methods helps identify flaws in the design of the classes. There are two different ways of measuring cohesion:

  1. Calculate for each data field in a class what percentage of methods use that data field. Average percentages then subtract from 100%. Lower percentages mean greater cohesion of data and methods in a class.
  2. High cohesion indicates good class subdivision. Lack of cohesion or low cohesion increases complexity, thereby increasing the likelihood of errors during the development process. The classes with low cohesion could probably be subdivided into two or more subclasses with increased cohesion.
